# Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF): Overview

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) is a type of heart failure in
which the heart's main pumping chamber, the left ventricle, squeezes (contracts)
normally but is too stiff to relax and fill with blood properly between beats. Because
the chamber cannot relax and fill the way it should, less blood is available to be
pumped out to the body, and pressure can build up and push fluid back into the lungs
and the rest of the body.

In HFpEF, the ejection fraction (the percentage of blood pumped out of the left
ventricle with each heartbeat) is preserved, meaning it is typically 50 percent or
higher. This is what separates HFpEF from heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
(HFrEF), where the ejection fraction is low. The "preserved" number can make HFpEF
harder to recognize because a normal-looking ejection fraction does not mean the heart
is working normally.

Common day-to-day symptoms of stable HFpEF include shortness of breath during physical
activity such as climbing stairs, getting tired easily during routine tasks, and mild
swelling (edema) in the ankles, feet, or legs caused by fluid building up. Some people
also notice that they need to prop themselves up with extra pillows to breathe
comfortably at night, or that they wake up feeling short of breath.

HFpEF is more common in older adults and in people who have long-standing high blood
pressure, diabetes, obesity, atrial fibrillation, or chronic kidney disease. Because
these conditions affect the heart over many years, HFpEF is often described as a
condition of the whole cardiovascular and metabolic system rather than the heart alone.

Diagnosis usually involves a combination of a physical exam, a review of symptoms,
blood tests (including markers such as natriuretic peptides), and imaging of the heart,
most commonly an echocardiogram. Only a qualified clinician can diagnose HFpEF, and the
information here is for general education and is not a diagnosis.
