# Safety Warnings: Emergency Symptoms and When to Seek Help

People living with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) and related
cardio-kidney-metabolic conditions should know the difference between routine changes
that can be managed with the care team and emergency symptoms that need immediate help.
This information is general education and does not replace professional medical advice.

Some symptoms are medical emergencies and require calling emergency services (such as 911
in Canada and the United States) or going to the nearest emergency department right away.
These include chest pain, chest pressure, or chest tightness; severe or sudden shortness
of breath; trouble breathing while at rest; fainting or passing out; severe or sudden
dizziness; a fast, pounding, or irregular heartbeat that does not settle; coughing up
pink or foamy fluid; bluish lips or fingertips; confusion or sudden difficulty speaking;
or weakness or numbness on one side of the body. These can be signs of a heart attack,
stroke, dangerous rhythm, or rapidly worsening heart failure, and waiting can be
dangerous.

Other symptoms are not usually emergencies but are warning signs that should prompt a
call to the care team within a day or so. These include a steady increase in swelling of
the legs or abdomen, a weight gain of more than about 2 to 3 pounds in a day or 5 pounds
in a week, needing more pillows than usual to sleep, increasing tiredness, or a gradual
reduction in the ability to do normal activities. The care team can advise whether a
medication change or an earlier visit is needed.

Medication safety is essential. Diuretic doses and other heart medicines should only be
changed under medical supervision. Patients should not stop, start, or adjust doses on
their own based on how they feel on a given day, because this can cause fluid overload or
dangerously low fluid levels. Over-the-counter medicines, including certain pain
relievers known as NSAIDs (for example ibuprofen and naproxen), and many herbal or
supplement products, can interfere with heart and kidney function or interact with
prescribed medicines, so a pharmacist or clinician should be asked before using them.

When in doubt about whether a symptom is an emergency, it is safer to seek urgent help. An
educational tool like this assistant cannot examine a patient, cannot diagnose, and must
never be used to decide against seeking emergency care. If symptoms are severe or
worsening quickly, contact emergency services immediately.
