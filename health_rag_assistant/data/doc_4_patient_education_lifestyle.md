# Patient Education: Daily Living and Self-Monitoring with HFpEF

Living well with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) involves daily
habits that help keep fluid balance steady and reduce strain on the heart. These habits
work alongside medicines, not instead of them, and they help people notice early when
something is changing.

Limiting salt (sodium) is one of the most important habits. Eating less salt helps the
body hold onto less water, which reduces swelling and breathlessness. Many care teams
suggest keeping sodium below about 2,000 milligrams per day, but the right target is set
by each person's clinician. Reading food labels, cooking at home, and limiting processed
and restaurant foods are practical ways to lower salt intake.

Daily weight monitoring is a simple but powerful tool. Weighing yourself at the same time
each morning, after using the bathroom and before eating, helps catch fluid buildup
early. A sudden weight gain of more than about 2 to 3 pounds in one day, or about 5
pounds in one week, can be a sign that fluid is accumulating and should be reported to
the care team, because it may mean a medication adjustment is needed. This is a warning
sign to call the clinic, not necessarily an emergency on its own.

Staying physically active in a way that is approved by the care team helps the heart and
overall health. Gentle, regular activity such as walking is often encouraged, with the
intensity matched to what each person can tolerate. Activity should be balanced with
rest, and people should pace themselves.

Other helpful daily habits include taking medicines on schedule, keeping all follow-up
appointments, managing other conditions such as diabetes and blood pressure, limiting
alcohol, avoiding tobacco, and getting recommended vaccinations to lower the risk of
infections that can stress the heart. Tracking symptoms in a simple diary can make
appointments more productive.

Knowing the difference between routine changes and warning signs is part of self-care.
Gradual changes such as slowly increasing swelling, needing more pillows to sleep, or
reduced ability to do usual activities are reasons to contact the care team for advice.
These are different from sudden, severe symptoms, which are emergencies and are covered in
the safety information.
