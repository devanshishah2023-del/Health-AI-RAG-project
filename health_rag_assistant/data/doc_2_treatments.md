# HFpEF Treatment Options and Medications

The management of heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) focuses on
controlling symptoms, treating the underlying conditions that drive it (such as high
blood pressure, diabetes, obesity, and kidney disease), and reducing the chance of being
hospitalized. There is no single cure; treatment is usually a combination of medication
and lifestyle changes that a care team adjusts over time.

A class of medicines called SGLT2 inhibitors, which includes empagliflozin and
dapagliflozin, is now a cornerstone of HFpEF treatment. Large clinical trials have shown
that these medicines reduce the risk of being hospitalized for heart failure and can
improve symptoms in people with HFpEF, even in those who do not have diabetes. They were
originally developed as diabetes medicines but are now used for their heart and kidney
benefits.

Diuretics, often called water pills, such as furosemide and other loop diuretics, are
commonly used to help the body remove extra salt and water through the kidneys. By
reducing fluid overload, diuretics can ease swelling in the legs and shortness of breath
caused by fluid in the lungs. The dose is tailored to each person and may change based on
weight, blood pressure, and kidney function.

Controlling blood pressure is a central part of HFpEF care because high blood pressure
makes the heart work harder and worsens stiffness over time. Medicines such as ACE
inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), the combination medicine
sacubitril-valsartan, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (such as
spironolactone) may be used in selected patients to manage blood pressure and reduce
strain on the heart. The right combination depends on the individual.

Treating related conditions matters as much as treating the heart directly. Good control
of diabetes, weight management, treatment of sleep apnea, and managing atrial
fibrillation (an irregular heart rhythm) all contribute to better outcomes in HFpEF.

All of these medicines should be taken exactly as prescribed. Patients should not start,
stop, or change the dose of any medication on their own. Questions about side effects,
new symptoms, or whether a treatment is working should be directed to the prescribing
clinician or pharmacist.

Good questions a patient can bring to a doctor's appointment include: Which of my
conditions is contributing most to my heart failure? Am I a candidate for an SGLT2
inhibitor? Is my blood pressure target right for me? Do any of my medicines interact
with each other? What symptoms should make me call the clinic versus go to the emergency
room?
