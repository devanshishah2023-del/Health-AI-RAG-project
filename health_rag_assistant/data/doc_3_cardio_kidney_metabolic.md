# Cardio-Kidney-Metabolic (CKM) Conditions and HFpEF

Cardio-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome describes the way that heart disease, kidney
disease, and metabolic conditions such as type 2 diabetes and obesity are connected and
tend to influence one another. In many people with heart failure with preserved ejection
fraction (HFpEF), these systems are affected together, which is why care teams look at
the whole picture rather than treating one organ in isolation.

The connection works in both directions. Excess weight and insulin resistance can raise
blood pressure and cause inflammation that stiffens the heart. High blood pressure and
diabetes can damage the small blood vessels in the kidneys, reducing kidney function.
When the kidneys cannot remove salt and water efficiently, fluid builds up, which
increases the workload on an already stiff heart. This cycle is part of why CKM
conditions and HFpEF so often appear together.

Kidney function is monitored using blood tests such as creatinine and the estimated
glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and urine tests that check for protein (albumin) in
the urine. These numbers help the care team choose medicines safely and adjust doses,
because some treatments depend on how well the kidneys are working.

Metabolic health is supported by managing blood sugar, blood pressure, and weight
together. Some modern medicines, including SGLT2 inhibitors and certain weight-management
medicines, can benefit the heart, kidneys, and metabolism at the same time, which is why
they are valuable in CKM care.

Because CKM conditions are interconnected, a small change in one area can affect the
others. For example, becoming dehydrated, eating a very high-salt meal, or skipping
medicines can shift fluid balance quickly. Regular follow-up and consistent daily habits
help keep the whole system stable. Specific targets and medicines should always be set by
the care team for the individual patient.
